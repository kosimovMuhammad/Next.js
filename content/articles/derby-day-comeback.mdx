Nobody inside the stadium believed it was coming back. Two goals down, reduced
to ten men since the 55th minute, and staring at a fourth straight derby
defeat — City's away end had already started filing toward the exits when the
comeback began.

## The turning point

A needless red card just after the hour should have ended the contest. Instead
it seemed to wake something up. The visiting manager threw on two fresh
attackers, switched to a back three, and dared the home side to keep attacking
into space that no longer existed.

It worked. A scrappy corner in the 81st minute was bundled home from close
range. Six minutes later, a mistimed offside trap gifted a one-on-one that was
finished with ice-cold composure.

## What it means

A draw rarely feels like a statement, but this one did. For a team written off
before kickoff, salvaging a point with ten men, away from home, in a fixture
that had gone against them for three years running, is the kind of result that
can shift a season's momentum.

The manager was careful not to overstate it after the final whistle: "One
point doesn't change the table. But it changes how this group believes in
itself."
