For years, the conventional wisdom was simple: play a big, physical striker
who can hold the ball up and win aerial duels, and build everything else
around him. That orthodoxy is cracking.

## What changed

Three of the league's top five attacks this season have started games with a
nominal striker who spends more time in midfield than the penalty area. The
effect is disorienting for defenses trained to track a fixed reference point —
when the "nine" drops deep, does the center-back follow into midfield, or hold
the line and open a gap in behind?

Most defenses are choosing wrong.

## The numbers

Teams using this approach are averaging 0.3 more expected goals per ninety
than they did with a traditional target man in the same fixtures last season.
The through-ball completion rate into the box has risen even more sharply,
since a false nine dragging a center-back out of position creates exactly the
space a running winger needs.

## Is it sustainable?

The obvious question is whether this is a genuine evolution or a fad that
disappears once defenses adjust. History suggests both are true at once —
the false nine will get countered eventually, and something else will take
its place. For now, though, it's the league's most effective attacking wrinkle.
